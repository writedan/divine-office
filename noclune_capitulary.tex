	\vechapter{Introduction}
		This book produces all the chapters and their attached responsories of the hours besides the Vigil, which lessons are contained in the lectionary. 
		
		The lector should, at the prescribed time, proceed to stand facing the altar between it and the nave, and proclaim the chapter there. Once finished, the congregation will respond:
		
		\skiplines{1}\gabcsnippet{(c3) <sp>r</sp> Thanks(h) be(h) to(h) God.(h)}
		
		When there is a prescribed responsory, it is intoned by either the lector (at the minor hours of Prime, Terce, Sext, None, and Compline) or by the minister (at Lauds and Vespers, as well as the Vigil). At the minor hours, the lector sings the response in full, which is then repeated by the congregation; at the major hours, the minister intones until the asterisk, then all the choir joins. In both cases, any verses are sung by the lector alone while the responses are sung by the congregation.
		
		The chapters of feria are given in \enquote{Ferial Days in Advent}, which follows the first Sunday in Advent. If deemed desirable, the chapter of a Sunday's second Vespers may be read throughout that week on ferial days without its responsory. However, once \enquote{O Wisdom} begins, the common ferial chapters should be used.
		
		The chapter of Lauds is usually reproduced as the chapter for Terce. It is occasionally reprinted, but oftentimes the instruction exists to read the chapter from Lauds. Lauds never has a responsory.
		
		The other chapters of the minor hours are usually from the same source of Scripture as that of Lauds and Terce. The minor hours always have a responsory.
		
		The chapter of Vespers varies. First Vespers usually produces its own chapter with the responsory either unique to itself or sourced from the Vigil. Second Vespers usually uses the same chapter as Lauds and a responsory associated with the liturgical season.
		
		Prime has a dominical and ferial chapter which is generally unchaging throughout the year. Compline likewise has a generally static chapter, but is more sensitive to the liturgical season than Prime. Their chapters are included in the following sections. However, Prime of Christmas is given in the temporale, which it is sung as until the octave of the Epiphany.
